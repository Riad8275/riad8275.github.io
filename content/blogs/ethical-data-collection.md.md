Introduction

Feedback plays an important role in improving education systems. Universities and colleges often rely on student surveys at the end of a course to understand what worked well and what needs improvement. These surveys are used by decision-makers to improve teaching quality, update course content, and enhance the overall student experience.
In this scenario, students are asked to complete a course evaluation survey through an online system. To access the survey, they must log in using their personal username and password. At the same time, the survey states that responses are anonymous and will only be used to improve courses.
At first glance, this may seem like a standard and harmless process. However, when examined more closely, it raises important ethical questions about privacy, transparency, trust, and the responsible use of data.
This blog explores the ethical concerns in the survey design, identifies additional risks, suggests improvements, and explains how a data analyst should respond if ethical recommendations are ignored.

Ethical Issues in the Survey Design

One of the main ethical concerns in this survey is the contradiction between requiring students to log in and claiming that responses are anonymous. When participants must authenticate themselves using a username and password, there is always a possibility that their responses could be linked back to them, even if names are not directly collected.
This creates concerns about privacy, which refers to an individual’s right to control how their personal information is collected and used. If students believe their responses are not truly anonymous, they may feel uncomfortable sharing honest opinions, especially negative feedback about courses or instructors.
Another major issue is transparency. Ethical data practices require organizations to clearly explain how data is collected, stored, and processed. In this case, students are not given enough information about whether login data is separated from survey responses or whether any identifying information is stored in the system.
There is also a concern about trust. Trust is essential in any data collection process. When students trust that their responses are confidential, they are more likely to provide honest and useful feedback. If they doubt anonymity, they may give safe or overly positive answers, which reduces the quality of the data.
In addition, the survey raises questions about informed consent. Participants should fully understand what they agree to before taking part in the survey. Without clear explanations about how anonymity is maintained, students cannot make a fully informed decision about their participation.
Overall, the design of the survey raises ethical concerns related to:
•	Privacy
•	Transparency
•	Trust
•	Informed consent

Additional Ethical Issues

Beyond the issue of anonymity, several other ethical risks may exist in this survey system.
One major concern is data security. Any online system that collects user data must protect it from unauthorized access. If survey responses and login credentials are stored in the same system, there is a risk that sensitive information could be exposed in case of a data breach.
Another issue is bias in responses. If students are worried that their answers could be traced back to them, they may not respond honestly. Instead, they might provide more positive feedback, which can distort the results and lead to inaccurate conclusions.
There are also concerns about fairness. Not all students may feel equally comfortable expressing criticism. This can lead to uneven participation and unbalanced data, where only certain voices are fully represented.
Other potential ethical risks include:
•	Misuse of data: Survey results could be used for purposes beyond course improvement, such as evaluating instructors or making administrative decisions that were not clearly communicated.
•	Weak data governance: There may be unclear rules about who can access the data, how long it is stored, and when it is deleted.
These risks highlight the importance of responsible data management practices throughout the entire lifecycle of collected information.

Recommendations for Executive Management

To address these ethical concerns, several improvements should be made to the survey system.
First, the organization should ensure that the survey is truly anonymous. If login is required to access the survey, the system should separate authentication from survey responses so that individual identities cannot be linked to answers.
Second, the institution should improve transparency by clearly explaining:
•	What data is collected
•	How it is stored
•	Who can access it
•	How long it will be kept
Third, stronger data security measures should be implemented. Access to survey data should be restricted to authorized personnel only, and appropriate safeguards should be in place to prevent data breaches.
Fourth, data use should be clearly defined. Survey results should only be used for their intended purpose—improving courses—unless additional uses are clearly communicated to participants.
Finally, the organization should regularly review its data collection practices to ensure they remain ethical and aligned with modern privacy expectations.

If Management Rejects the Recommendations

If executive management refuses to accept these recommendations and insists that no ethical issues exist, a data analyst still has a professional responsibility to act ethically.
The first step would be to document all concerns and recommendations. Keeping a written record ensures that ethical issues were properly identified and communicated.
Next, the analyst should continue to raise concerns in a respectful and professional manner. Decision-makers may not fully understand the impact of privacy risks or the importance of transparency, so further explanation may still be helpful.
If the issue is not resolved, the analyst should follow formal reporting channels such as supervisors, privacy officers, or ethics committees within the organization.
When presenting results, the analyst should also clearly communicate any limitations in the data. For example, if anonymity concerns may affect response honesty, this should be included in any analysis or reporting.
Most importantly, the analyst must maintain professional integrity. Ethical responsibility is not only about following instructions but also about protecting the individuals whose data is being collected. Acting with honesty, fairness, and accountability ensures that professional standards are upheld even in difficult situations.

Conclusion

Although student surveys are a common and useful tool for improving education, they must be designed and managed ethically. In this case, requiring students to log in while claiming anonymity creates concerns about privacy, transparency, trust, and informed consent.
Additional risks such as data security issues, bias, fairness concerns, and unclear data governance further highlight the importance of responsible data practices.
By improving transparency, strengthening privacy protections, and ensuring proper data governance, organizations can collect more accurate and trustworthy feedback while respecting student rights.
Ultimately, ethical data collection is not just about following rules; it is about building trust and ensuring that data is used in a fair, responsible, and meaningful way.
